### L15 (NEW — CODIFY): Check pending corporate transactions before any EPS prediction

**Origin:** HPE Q2 FY26 GRADE 2026-06-02. Non-GAAP EPS $0.79 vs my $0.57 point estimate (+38% miss). The primary EPS gap driver was H3C divestiture completion on May 28, 2026 — 3 days before HPE's Q2 print. Total pretax H3C consideration ~$3.5B. **Generalizable lesson:** Before any EPS prediction for a multi-segment or multi-entity company, explicitly check SEC EDGAR (or company investor page) for: (a) any announced-but-not-yet-closed M&A transaction, (b) any pending divestiture with known timing, (c) any asset monetization with Q-specific cash recognition. These are PUBLIC data points that cause material EPS deviations from operations-only models. The failure mode is treating the company as a pure operating entity when a balance-sheet event is about to close.

**Which layer failed:** INPUT — the data was publicly available; I did not check it.

**Calibration adjustment:** Add "corporate event check" as a mandatory step in the PREDICT workflow input validation. Specifically: before finalizing any EPS estimate, run a 30-second SEC EDGAR check for the target company's most recent 8-K filings in the prior 90 days. Look for: "divestiture," "acquisition closing," "asset sale," "proceeds received," "consideration received." If any is pending or recently closed, model the non-operating EPS impact separately.

**Falsification criterion:** if next 3 EPS predictions include the corporate-event check AND produce calibrated EPS calls (no material non-operating EPS gap unexplained), L15 is confirmed as a workflow discipline.

### L16 (NEW — CANDIDATE, N=1): For cohort backlog signal, specify the sub-mechanism (accumulate vs convert) before applying

**Origin:** HPE Q2 FY26 GRADE 2026-06-02. Predicted AI backlog $7-9B using DELL's +19% sequential backlog accumulation pattern. Actual $5.9B (+18%) — correct % growth but wrong absolute level. The mechanism: DELL was in demand-exceeds-shipment (backlog-accumulating) mode; HPE was in shipment-exceeding-incremental-orders (backlog-converting) mode at scale.

**Generalizable lesson:** When applying a cohort's backlog signal, the first question is "is the cohort company ACCUMULATING backlog (orders > shipments) or CONVERTING it (shipments > orders)?" These produce opposite backlog trajectories from the same underlying demand environment. A company that is shipping through its backlog rapidly will show LOWER sequential backlog growth than a company that is building backlog — even if underlying AI demand is identical.

**Calibration adjustment:** For future predictions involving AI backlog metrics, explicitly label the target's current operational mode: (a) ACCUMULATION mode — apply DELL-style sequential % growth; (b) CONVERSION mode — expected flat or modest growth even in strong demand. The mode is inferred from: prior-quarter earnings call language about delivery timing, AI systems orders-vs-shipments ratio, and whether the company has disclosed strong shipment pacing language.

**Falsification criterion:** if next 2+ companies with known CONVERSION mode show backlog growth rates that match an ACCUMULATION cohort's pattern, L16 was wrong about the sub-mechanism distinction.
